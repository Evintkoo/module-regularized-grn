Regulatory Network Inference: An Empirical Comparison Under Parameter-Matched
Conditions}

\author{Evint Leovonzko%
\thanks{E-mail: evint.koo@gmail.com. Independent researcher.
Code: \protect\url{https://github.com/evintleovonzko/module-regularized-grn}.}}

\begin{abstract}
Gene regulatory network (GRN) inference---determining which transcription factors
regulate which target genes from single-cell RNA-sequencing data---is a core
problem in computational biology. We present a parameter-matched comparison of
dual-encoder (two-tower) and cross-encoder MLPs on a human brain
single-nucleus RNA-seq dataset (39K TF--gene pairs, 5.58M parameters each)
under balanced (1:1) and moderately imbalanced (5:1) negative-to-positive
sampling ratios. The cross-encoder achieves AUROC 0.9025 versus 0.7941 for
the two-tower under balanced training---a gap of 10.8 points consistent across
five random seeds ($t(4)=27.08$, $p<0.0001$, Bonferroni-corrected
$\alpha=0.017$, Cohen's $d=12.1$). A no-product cross-encoder ablation
attributes $\approx$95\% of this gap to joint encoding and $\approx$5\% to
the multiplicative interaction term. Under 5:1 negative sampling, the
two-tower AUROC degrades by 10.1 points while the cross-encoder remains
stable; both architectures exhibit F1 degradation. Structured neuron pruning
across three random seeds shows the two-tower maintains post-hoc AUROC
retention $\geq 0.997$ up to 60\% sparsity, with mean retention falling to
0.978 at 90\% sparsity; the cross-encoder shows comparable post-hoc stability
through 70\% sparsity. Both architectures operate in a transductive setting
(entity embeddings are learned from the training split and cannot generalize
to unseen TFs or genes), and the evaluation task measures reconstruction of
curated regulatory databases rather than inference of novel regulation. All
findings are on a single human brain dataset and are qualified to that
experimental context; comparison to established GRN inference baselines
(GENIE3, SCENIC) is a necessary next step.
\end{abstract}

% ── I. INTRODUCTION ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Gene regulatory network (GRN) inference --- the problem of identifying which
transcription factors (TFs) regulate which target genes --- is a fundamental
challenge in computational biology. Understanding transcriptional regulatory
relationships is essential for deciphering developmental programs, disease
mechanisms, and therapeutic targets. With the availability of large-scale
single-nucleus RNA-sequencing (snRNA-seq) data, machine learning methods have
emerged that cast GRN inference as binary link prediction: given a candidate
TF--gene pair and their expression profiles, predict whether a regulatory
relationship exists~\cite{aibar2017,huynh2010}.

A central architectural question in link prediction is whether entity
representations should be computed independently (a \emph{dual-encoder} or
two-tower model~\cite{huang2013,bromley1993}) or jointly (a
\emph{cross-encoder}~\cite{nogueira2019,humeau2020}). Two-tower models score
interactions via cosine similarity of independently computed encodings,
enabling efficient inference at scale. Cross-encoders process pairs jointly,
allowing arbitrary interaction features to be learned before scoring. In
information retrieval, cross-encoders consistently outperform two-tower models
when precise ranking quality is needed~\cite{nogueira2019,humeau2020}, but
whether this advantage holds in computational biology---where entities have
distinct biological identities and the regulatory signal is low-dimensional---has
not been established under controlled, parameter-matched conditions.

Three open questions motivate this work: (1)~Does a cross-encoder achieve
higher discriminative AUROC than a parameter-matched two-tower for TF--gene
link prediction? (2)~Is the two-tower's cosine similarity scoring more
sensitive to class imbalance than the cross-encoder's MLP scoring?
(3)~Does the trained two-tower actually utilize its nominal hidden-layer
capacity, or is much of it redundant?

This paper makes three contributions:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{C1:} A parameter-matched comparison (5.58M parameters each)
  of two-tower and cross-encoder MLPs on human brain snRNA-seq data under
  both balanced (1:1) and moderately imbalanced (5:1) negative-to-positive
  sampling ratios, including an ablation of the cross-encoder without its
  element-wise interaction term. The ablation isolates the contribution of
  joint encoding per se: the no-product cross-encoder achieves mean AUROC
  0.8972 (1:1) versus 0.9025 for the full cross-encoder and 0.7941 for the
  two-tower, attributing $\approx$95\% of the 10.8-point gap to joint encoding
  and $\approx$5\% to the element-wise product term
  (see Section~\ref{ssec:ablation}).
\item \textbf{C2:} Demonstration that the two-tower degrades by 10.1~AUROC
  points under moderately imbalanced (5:1) negative sampling
  ($t(4.25)=4.06$, $p=0.014$) while the cross-encoder AUROC is stable (F1
  degrades for both architectures); the true TF--gene imbalance is
  $\approx$376:1 and constitutes an open question.
\item \textbf{C3:} A structured neuron pruning analysis across three random
  seeds showing that post-hoc AUROC retention is $\geq 0.997$ (mean across
  seeds) up to 60\% sparsity for the two-tower, declining to 0.978 at 90\%
  sparsity. The same protocol applied to the cross-encoder shows post-hoc
  retention $\geq 0.999$ through 70\% sparsity, dropping to 0.987 at 90\%,
  demonstrating that representational redundancy is not exclusive to cosine
  similarity scoring.
\end{itemize}

The remainder of this paper is organized as follows.
Section~\ref{sec:related} reviews related work.
Section~\ref{sec:methods} describes the dataset, architectures, training
protocol, ablation design, and pruning procedure.
Section~\ref{sec:results} presents experimental results, including the
element-wise product ablation, cross-encoder pruning, and temperature
sensitivity analysis.
Section~\ref{sec:discussion} discusses implications and limitations.
Section~\ref{sec:conclusion} concludes.

% ── II. RELATED WORK ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\subsection{Gene Regulatory Network Inference as Link Prediction}

Gene regulatory network inference is the problem of identifying directed edges
in a graph where nodes are genes and edges represent transcriptional
regulation. Curated databases such as DoRothEA~\cite{garcia2019} and
TRRUST~\cite{han2018} provide partial ground truth derived from ChIP-seq
experiments and literature curation, but coverage is sparse relative to the
space of possible regulatory interactions.

Machine learning approaches treat the problem as binary classification over
TF--gene pairs. Early methods such as GENIE3~\cite{huynh2010} used random
forest feature importance to rank TF--gene interactions. SCENIC~\cite{aibar2017}
extended this with cis-regulatory motif analysis. GRNBoost2~\cite{moerman2019}
improved upon GENIE3 with gradient boosting, achieving substantially faster
runtimes with comparable accuracy. Information-theoretic methods such as
PIDC~\cite{chan2017} exploit multivariate mutual information to capture
higher-order regulatory dependencies that pairwise correlation measures miss.
More recent deep learning approaches learn representations of TFs and genes
directly from expression profiles to score candidate edges.

The scale of the problem is biologically daunting: the human genome encodes
approximately 1,600 transcription factors~\cite{lambert2018}, each potentially
regulating hundreds to thousands of target genes. Exhaustive experimental
characterization is infeasible, and curated databases cover only a small
fraction of the regulatory landscape---making the quality of negative labels
a persistent concern, since pairs absent from databases may represent
undiscovered regulatory relationships rather than true negatives.

\subsection{Two-Tower (Dual-Encoder) Architectures}

The two-tower paradigm was introduced for web search in the Deep Structured
Semantic Model (DSSM)~\cite{huang2013}, where separate networks encode query
and document into a shared embedding space and relevance is scored by cosine
similarity. This enables efficient large-scale retrieval: all entity
embeddings can be pre-computed and indexed, reducing per-query cost from
$O(Nd)$ for an MLP to $O(d)$ where $d$ is the embedding dimension. Dense
Passage Retrieval (DPR)~\cite{karpukhin2020} demonstrated the practical power
of this approach at scale: pre-computed passage embeddings indexed with
approximate nearest-neighbor search enable millisecond-latency retrieval over
millions of documents, a property highly relevant for genome-scale GRN
inference where the TF$\times$gene product space can reach $10^8$ candidate pairs.

Siamese networks~\cite{bromley1993} represent the closely related case where
the two towers share weights. In the GRN setting, TF and gene encoders are
kept separate (non-Siamese) to allow distinct representation spaces for the
two biological entity types.

The key limitation of the two-tower architecture is the
\emph{representation bottleneck}: all information needed to compute a
regulatory score must be compressed into independent fixed-dimensional
encodings before any interaction is possible. This precludes learning
features that are inherently joint --- for example, regulatory interactions
predictable only from the simultaneous observation of TF expression level and
gene chromatin accessibility.

\subsection{Cross-Encoder Architectures}

Cross-encoders process pairs jointly as a single concatenated input, allowing
the model to learn arbitrary interaction features before the final scoring
layer. Nogueira and Cho~\cite{nogueira2019} demonstrated that cross-encoders
substantially outperform bi-encoders on reranking tasks where precise
relevance judgements are needed, at the cost of requiring per-pair computation
at inference time. Humeau et al.~\cite{humeau2020} further characterized this
trade-off in Poly-encoders, and Khattab and Zaharia~\cite{khattab2020}
explored late interaction in ColBERT as a hybrid approach. These works
collectively establish that joint encoding is more expressive than factorized
encoding. In natural language processing, BERT~\cite{devlin2019} is the
dominant cross-encoder backbone: its transformer self-attention mechanism
allows every token in the query to attend to every token in the document
before the scoring layer, achieving deep pairwise interaction at the cost of
$O(N^2)$ per-pair computation. The MLP cross-encoder used in this work adopts
the same inductive bias---joint input processing before scoring---without
the quadratic attention cost, which is tractable for fixed-length embedding
inputs.

The element-wise product $\mathbf{a} \odot \mathbf{b}$ of two embeddings is
a standard feature engineering technique that provides a direct path for
learning which embedding dimensions co-activate for relevant
pairs~\cite{rendle2010}.

\subsection{Structured Pruning and Representational Redundancy}

Neural network pruning removes parameters from a trained network to reduce
model size or inference cost while preserving performance. Unstructured pruning
removes individual weights~\cite{han2015}; structured pruning removes entire
neurons or filters, producing actual speedups without sparse-matrix libraries.
The earliest principled pruning criterion, Optimal Brain Damage~\cite{lecun1990},
used second-order Taylor expansions to identify and remove low-saliency
weights; this saliency-based perspective underpins many modern importance
scores including the activation-frequency and weight-magnitude criteria used
in this work.

Han et al.~\cite{han2015} demonstrated that large networks tolerate removal
of 80--90\% of weights with minimal accuracy loss, suggesting that trained
networks are highly over-parameterized. The Lottery Ticket
Hypothesis~\cite{frankle2019} further showed that sparse sub-networks exist
within trained networks that can be trained in isolation to match the full
network, implying that useful representational capacity is localized.
A comprehensive survey of pruning methodology~\cite{blalock2020} found that
reported results across the literature are difficult to compare due to
inconsistencies in baselines, fine-tuning budgets, and sparsity definitions;
it recommends reporting both immediate post-pruning and fine-tuned
performance at each sparsity level---a convention this paper follows.

% ── III. METHODS ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Methods}
\label{sec:methods}

\subsection{Dataset and Prior Knowledge}
\label{ssec:data}

Gene expression data were obtained from a human brain single-nucleus RNA-seq
dataset (CellxGene Census accession \texttt{fe1a73ab-a203-45fd-84e9-0f7fd19efcbd};
human, \textit{Homo sapiens}, brain tissue).
Cell-type-level expression profiles were summarized to
11 cell-type dimensions representing mean expression across major brain cell
types (neurons, glia, oligodendrocytes, microglia, endothelial cells, and
subtypes thereof).  Only 2{,}000 highly variable genes were retained after
preprocessing; expression coverage for the 1{,}164 TFs and 7{,}664 genes
used in the model is 10.7\% and 14.6\%, respectively, with all remaining
entities assigned a zero expression vector.

Prior regulatory knowledge was sourced from merged DoRothEA~\cite{garcia2019}
and TRRUST~\cite{han2018} databases, yielding approximately 29,000 TF--gene
pairs as candidate positive examples. After filtering for TFs and genes with
available expression data, approximately 23,700 positive pairs are retained.
An equal number of negative (non-regulatory) examples were sampled uniformly
from the space of TF--gene pairs not present in the prior databases, producing
a balanced dataset of approximately 47,400 total examples. The dataset was
split into training (70\%), validation (15\%), and test (15\%) sets using a
fixed random seed (42) applied once before any model training, ensuring that
all model comparisons use identical data splits (test set: 7,109 examples).
Note that the space of possible TF--gene pairs is $1{,}164 \times 7{,}664
\approx 8.9$~million; the true non-edge to edge ratio is therefore
approximately 376:1, far more severe than any ratio tested here. The human
genome encodes approximately 1,600 transcription factors in total~\cite{lambert2018};
the 1,164 TFs retained here represent those for which both prior-knowledge
annotations and expression data are available in this dataset.

\textbf{Transductive learning setting.}
Both architectures learn a dense embedding vector for each of the 1,164 TFs
and 7,664 genes encountered during training. These embedding parameters
(4.52M of the total 5.58M) are model weights that are only defined for
entities present in the training vocabulary. The evaluation therefore
operates in a \emph{transductive} setting: test-set TF--gene pairs involve
the same TFs and genes seen during training, and the model cannot produce
predictions for entities outside its training vocabulary without retraining.
This is a fundamental limitation for practical deployment in new tissues or
organisms. Inductive extensions (e.g., using only expression features or
pre-trained biological language model embeddings as fixed inputs) would be
required to generalize to novel entities.

\textbf{Task framing and label quality.}
Positive labels are sourced exclusively from DoRothEA and TRRUST; negative
labels are sampled from the complement of those databases. The
evaluation therefore measures how well a model \emph{reconstructs the
curated database} from expression features and learned entity embeddings,
not whether it can discover novel regulatory relationships. Database coverage
is sparse: only $\approx$0.27\% of the 8.9~million possible TF--gene pairs
are annotated as positive. Many true regulatory relationships are absent from
these databases (false negatives), and the contamination rate is not
quantified. As an upper-bound estimate of expression-feature informativeness
for this task, a gradient-boosted tree classifier using only the 22-dimensional
expression-feature representation (11-dim cell-type profiles per entity)
achieves AUROC~0.587 on the same test split---barely above chance---confirming
that the neural models' higher AUROC is driven primarily by the learned entity
embeddings encoding database membership structure, not by expression-based
regulatory signal.

\subsection{Model Architectures}
\label{ssec:arch}

\textbf{Two-Tower MLP.} The two-tower architecture encodes TF and gene
representations independently before scoring. Each entity is represented by
the concatenation of a learned 512-dimensional embedding and an 11-dimensional
cell-type expression profile, yielding a 523-dimensional input per entity.
This input is passed through two fully connected layers with 512 hidden units
and ReLU activation, producing a 512-dimensional output encoding. Regulatory
scores are computed as the cosine similarity of the TF and gene encodings,
scaled by a temperature parameter $\tau = 0.05$ and passed through a sigmoid
function to produce probabilities. The total parameter count is 5,581,824,
dominated by the embedding tables (1,164 TFs and 7,664 genes at 512 dimensions
each).

\textbf{Cross-Encoder MLP.} The cross-encoder receives TF and gene features
jointly. The input is formed by concatenating: the TF embedding (512d), the
gene embedding (512d), their element-wise product (512d), the TF expression
profile (11d), and the gene expression profile (11d), yielding a
1,558-dimensional joint representation. This is passed through two fully
connected layers of 512 units with ReLU activation, followed by a linear
output layer mapping to a scalar logit, which is passed through sigmoid to
produce a probability. The total parameter count is 5,581,313 --- effectively
identical to the two-tower (ratio = 1.000), ensuring a parameter-matched
comparison.

Both architectures were implemented from scratch in pure Rust using the ndarray
library, without dependence on PyTorch, TensorFlow, or any external machine
learning framework. Fig.~\ref{fig:arch} illustrates the two architectures side
by side. Code is available at \url{https://github.com/evintleovonzko/module-regularized-grn}.

\textbf{Note on comparability.} The cross-encoder input includes an
element-wise product term ($\mathbf{e}_{TF} \odot \mathbf{e}_{g}$) that
encodes pairwise multiplicative interactions as an inductive bias absent from
the two-tower. To isolate the contribution of joint encoding from this
additional feature, we trained a \emph{no-product cross-encoder} ablation
(input: $[\mathbf{e}_{TF} \,|\, \mathbf{e}_g \,|\, \mathbf{x}_{TF} \,|\,
\mathbf{x}_g]$, $2d + 2m$ dimensions) under identical conditions. The
no-product cross-encoder has 5.32M parameters---slightly fewer than the
full CE and two-tower (5.58M each) due to the reduced fc1 input
dimension; this modest parameter difference ($\approx$4.8\%) is noted as a
residual confound in the attribution. Results are
reported in Section~\ref{ssec:ablation}; the element-wise product contributes
$\approx$5\% of the total AUROC gap, with joint encoding accounting for the
remaining $\approx$95\%.

\subsection{Training Protocol}
\label{ssec:training}

All models were trained with the Adam optimizer~\cite{kingma2014} with learning
rate $\eta = 0.001$, $\beta_1 = 0.9$, $\beta_2 = 0.999$, and gradient
clipping at a global norm of 5.0. The batch size was 256 examples. The
learning rate $\eta = 0.001$ was selected based on comparative stability
across architectures during preliminary training runs; $\eta = 0.005$ produced
faster but less stable convergence for the cross-encoder. To ensure comparable
optimization conditions, both architectures were trained at the lower value.
Note that the optimal learning rate for each architecture independently was
not determined via grid search; this choice and its implications for the
architectural comparison are discussed in Section~\ref{sec:discussion}.
Training ran for a maximum of 60 epochs with early stopping: validation
accuracy was evaluated every 10 epochs, and training was halted if no
improvement was observed over 10 consecutive evaluations. The loss function was
binary cross-entropy with gradient computed in logit space, $\partial
\mathrm{BCE} / \partial \mathrm{logit} = (p - l) / N_\text{batch}$, which is
numerically stable. All weights were initialized as follows: fully connected layer weights use
He initialization ($\sigma = \sqrt{2/\text{fan\_in}}$ with uniform sampling
over $[-\sigma, \sigma]$, matching the ReLU activation); biases are initialized
to zero; embedding tables use $\mathcal{N}(0, 0.01)$, a small-variance normal
appropriate for embedding lookup tables.

\subsection{Evaluation}
\label{ssec:eval}

All models were evaluated on the held-out test set (7,109 examples) using four
metrics: accuracy (fraction correctly classified at a fixed decision threshold
of 0.5), area under the receiver operating characteristic curve (AUROC), macro
F1 score (computed at the same fixed threshold of 0.5), and area under the
precision-recall curve (AUPRC). Each
model configuration was trained with five random seeds (42, 123, 456, 789,
1337), and results are reported as mean $\pm$ standard deviation across seeds.
Bootstrap 95\% confidence intervals for accuracy were computed via 1{,}000
stratified resamples of the test set, applied independently to all five seeds
and reported as the mean of the per-seed interval bounds; this characterizes
architectural-level uncertainty rather than single-seed sampling variance.
Effect sizes are reported as Cohen's $d$ computed from the per-seed
distribution. All metrics are reported to four decimal places for consistency. An
ensemble prediction was also computed as the mean prediction across all five
seeds, providing an upper-bound accuracy and AUROC estimate.

\textbf{Multiple-testing correction.}
Three primary hypothesis tests are reported (C1 paired $t$-test, C2 Welch
$t$-test, and the no-product ablation $t$-test). Bonferroni-corrected
significance threshold is $\alpha_{\text{adj}} = 0.05/3 \approx 0.017$.
All three reported tests meet this threshold: C1 ($p<0.0001$), C2 ($p=0.014$,
marginal), and ablation ($p=0.015$, marginal). Readers should interpret C2 and
the ablation $t$-test as nominally significant under Bonferroni correction;
replication on additional datasets is required to confirm these effects.

\subsection{Negative Sampling}
\label{ssec:negsampling}

Two negative sampling regimes were evaluated: balanced (1:1), with equal
numbers of positive and negative examples, matching the typical assumption in
benchmark evaluations of GRN inference methods; and moderately imbalanced
(5:1), with five negative examples per positive example, providing a
controlled test of robustness to class imbalance. Note that the 5:1 ratio is
far from a true ecologically valid regime: with $\approx$23,700 retained
positive pairs against $\approx$8.9~million possible TF--gene pairs, the
true ratio is approximately 376:1. The 5:1 condition is therefore a
tractable intermediate step; results under severe imbalance
(${\geq}$50:1) are not established here and are noted as a limitation
(Section~\ref{sec:discussion}).

\subsection{Neuron Pruning Protocol}
\label{ssec:pruning}

To assess representational redundancy in trained models, a structured neuron
pruning experiment was conducted across three random seeds (42, 123, 456) for
the two-tower and one seed (42) for the cross-encoder.\footnote{The pruning
baseline AUROC values differ slightly from the per-seed values in
Table~\ref{tab:perseed} because the pruning experiment re-trains each model
independently from the same seed rather than loading a saved checkpoint from
the main evaluation run. Stochastic variation across training runs accounts
for the small discrepancy; the pruning table and main tables use
self-consistent baselines within their respective contexts.}
Neuron importance was scored for each of the 512 output neurons
in each tower's first fully connected layer (fc1) as:
%
\begin{align}
  \text{importance}(j) &= \alpha \cdot \text{activation\_freq}(j) \nonumber \\
                       &\quad + (1 - \alpha) \cdot \text{weight\_magnitude}(j)
\end{align}
%
where $\alpha = 0.5$ (equal weighting), $\text{activation\_freq}(j)$ is the
fraction of training examples for which neuron $j$'s post-ReLU activation
exceeds zero, and $\text{weight\_magnitude}(j)$ is the mean of the normalized
L2 norms of column $j$ of the fc1 weight matrix and row $j$ of the fc2 weight
matrix. Normalization to $[0,1]$ was applied independently per tower before
combining.

Pruning was applied at 13 sparsity levels (0\%, 5\%, 10\%, 15\%, 20\%, 25\%,
30\%, 40\%, 50\%, 60\%, 70\%, 80\%, and 90\%). At each level, the
lowest-importance neurons were removed by deleting the corresponding columns
from fc1's weight matrix and rows from fc2's weight matrix. Each sparsity level
was evaluated as an independent branch from the same trained baseline, with no
cumulative pruning across levels.

Two evaluations were performed at each sparsity level: (1)~a \emph{post-hoc}
evaluation immediately after pruning (no additional training), and
(2)~a \emph{fine-tuned} evaluation after 10 epochs of continued training with
a fresh Adam optimizer state at the same learning rate. AUROC retention was
defined as the ratio of pruned model AUROC to baseline AUROC.

\subsection{Ablation Protocol}
\label{ssec:ablation_method}

To isolate the contribution of joint encoding from the element-wise product
inductive bias in the cross-encoder, a \emph{no-product cross-encoder} was
constructed by removing the $\mathbf{e}_{TF} \odot \mathbf{e}_g$ term from
the input. The resulting model receives
$[\mathbf{e}_{TF} \,|\, \mathbf{e}_g \,|\, \mathbf{x}_{TF} \,|\, \mathbf{x}_g]$
as input ($2d + 2m = 1{,}046$ dimensions) and has 5.44M parameters, slightly
fewer than the two-tower and full cross-encoder due to the reduced fc1 input
dimension. All other hyperparameters (learning rate, batch size, epochs, seed
list, early stopping) are identical to the main cross-encoder training. Both
the 1:1 and 5:1 ratios were evaluated with five seeds (42, 123, 456, 789,
1337).

\subsection{Temperature Grid Search}
\label{ssec:tempsearch_method}

To verify that $\tau = 0.05$ is not suboptimal for the two-tower, five
temperature values were evaluated: $\tau \in \{0.01, 0.05, 0.1, 0.5, 1.0\}$.
For each $\tau$, the two-tower was trained from scratch using seeds 42, 123,
and 456 at the 1:1 ratio, and mean AUROC $\pm$ std was recorded.

% ── IV. RESULTS ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}

\subsection{Cross-Encoder vs.\ Two-Tower Under Balanced Training}
\label{ssec:h1}

Table~\ref{tab:balanced} presents the performance of both architectures at the
1:1 negative sampling ratio across five random seeds.

The cross-encoder achieves an AUROC of 0.9025, compared to 0.7941 for the
two-tower---a difference of 10.84 AUROC points. This gap is consistent across
all five seeds (Table~\ref{tab:perseed}) and is the primary evidence supporting
C1.

Bootstrap 95\% confidence intervals for accuracy (computed as the mean of
per-seed interval bounds across all five seeds) are: Cross-Encoder
[82.75\%,~84.43\%], Two-Tower [77.66\%,~79.58\%]. These intervals do not
overlap, cleanly separating the two models in terms of accuracy. The AUROC
gap is larger and more decisive: AUROC measures the model's ability to rank
positive edges above negative edges across all possible decision thresholds,
and the cross-encoder substantially outperforms the two-tower on this metric.
A paired $t$-test on per-seed AUROC differences confirms strong statistical
significance: mean difference $= 0.1084$,
$t(4) = 27.08$, $p < 0.0001$ (two-tailed, Bonferroni-corrected
$\alpha = 0.017$), Cohen's $d = 12.1$.

The cross-encoder also exhibits markedly lower variance across seeds
(std $= 0.0007$ vs.\ $0.0096$), suggesting considerably more stable
optimization. Ensemble accuracy narrows the raw advantage
(84.43\% vs.\ 82.49\%); ensemble AUROC shows a similar pattern
(0.9114 vs.\ 0.8778), consistent with ensemble averaging reducing the effect
of seed-specific variance.
Fig.~\ref{fig:auroc} summarizes the AUROC results for all four experimental
conditions.

\textbf{Interpretation.} The cross-encoder's AUROC advantage is attributable to
its joint input representation. By concatenating TF embeddings, gene embeddings,
and their element-wise product before the first hidden layer, the cross-encoder
can learn features that are intrinsically relational --- for example, that
certain embedding subspaces of TF and gene jointly predict regulation in a way
that is not recoverable from either embedding alone. The two-tower, constrained
to score interactions via cosine similarity of independently computed encodings,
cannot represent such features: its representational bottleneck enforces that
all information relevant to scoring must be independently extractable from TF
and gene inputs before any interaction occurs.

The AUROC gap is more pronounced than the accuracy gap because accuracy is
measured at a fixed threshold of 0.5, while AUROC integrates over all
thresholds and provides a more sensitive measure of ranking quality---the
appropriate metric for comparing models that differ in their confidence
calibration. These results support C1.

\subsection{Robustness to Class Imbalance}
\label{ssec:h2}

Table~\ref{tab:imbalance} presents the performance of both architectures at
both negative sampling ratios, allowing direct comparison of the degradation
incurred by each model when moving from balanced to moderately imbalanced
training conditions.

The two-tower AUROC degrades by 10.1 points under 5:1 sampling
(0.7941~$\to$~0.6930), while the cross-encoder AUROC marginally improves by
1.0 point (0.9025~$\to$~0.9121). The two-tower F1 collapses by 23.3 points
(0.7962~$\to$~0.5630), indicating the model loses its ability to maintain
precision at adequate recall when negatives dominate training. Training variance
for the two-tower increases 5.7$\times$ (std $= 0.0096~\to~0.0548$),
reflecting substantially less stable optimization at the higher negative ratio.
A Welch two-sample $t$-test on per-group AUROC confirms the significance of
the two-tower degradation: $t(4.25) = 4.06$, $p = 0.014$ (two-tailed,
Bonferroni-corrected $\alpha = 0.017$; marginally significant under
correction); Welch's unequal-variance form is appropriate given the
5.7$\times$ increase in variance under 5:1
sampling.\footnote{Welch--Satterthwaite effective
degrees of freedom: $\nu = (s_1^2/n + s_2^2/n)^2 / [(s_1^2/n)^2/(n{-}1) +
(s_2^2/n)^2/(n{-}1)]$ where $s_1 = 0.0096$, $s_2 = 0.0548$,
$n = 5$, yielding $\nu \approx 4.25$.}

\textbf{Note on test-set distribution.}
The 1:1 and 5:1 models are both evaluated on the same balanced (1:1) held-out
test set: additional negatives for the 5:1 condition are added to the training
set only. AUROC is threshold-independent and valid under this protocol;
however, accuracy and F1 at the fixed 0.5 threshold are affected by the
training-distribution shift, because models trained at 5:1 are miscalibrated
relative to the balanced test prior. The AUROC values are therefore the
primary basis for comparing the two conditions.

The cross-encoder is not immune to imbalance effects: its F1 score degrades by
4.1 points (0.8338~$\to$~0.7929) as recall is harder to maintain with a larger
negative class. However, its AUROC is unaffected, confirming that its
discriminative capacity --- the ability to rank positive edges above negatives
--- is preserved under the 5:1 imbalanced condition.

\textbf{Interpretation.} The two-tower's geometric scoring mechanism is
intrinsically sensitive to the marginal distribution of embeddings in the shared
representation space. During training with 5$\times$ more negative examples,
the Adam optimizer updates the TF and gene embedding tables primarily to
separate the mass of negative pairs, distorting the embedding geometry and
disrupting the alignment of positive TF--gene pairs that would otherwise support
high AUROC. The cosine similarity score is a global measure of directional
similarity; if the typical negative pair occupies a large angular region of the
embedding space, the model must expand that region's dissimilarity at the cost
of compressing the positive region.

The cross-encoder does not face this problem because it learns to distinguish
positive from negative pairs through local feature interactions rather than
global geometric separation. Its MLP scoring function can weight the
element-wise product and individual embedding features differently for each
pair, allowing it to learn decision boundaries that are robust to changes in
the class distribution during training.

The marginal AUROC \emph{improvement} of the cross-encoder under 5:1 sampling
(0.9025~$\to$~0.9121) warrants specific comment. An increased proportion of
negative training examples may act as a regularizer for the cross-encoder's
MLP readout: the additional hard negatives sharpen the decision boundary and
reduce false positives, improving ranking ability. This is consistent with
the simultaneous F1 degradation (0.8338~$\to$~0.7929): the model's
threshold-independent ranking improves slightly while its threshold-dependent
binary classification degrades because the optimal decision threshold shifts
with the changed class prior. This interpretation is speculative; confirming
it would require ablations that vary the class prior while holding out
balanced test data.

This result has direct practical implications. Curated regulatory databases
such as DoRothEA and TRRUST cover at most a few percent of possible TF--gene
pairs in the human genome; realistic GRN inference operates under
class imbalance far more severe than 5:1 (approximately 376:1 for the
dataset used here). The cross-encoder's stability under the 5:1 condition
--- and its further AUROC improvement --- suggests it is a more
deployment-appropriate architecture; its behavior under true genome-scale
imbalance remains to be established. These results support C2.

\textbf{Connection to the imbalanced learning literature.} Class imbalance
is a long-studied challenge in machine learning, and a rich body of
remediation strategies exists~\cite{he2009}. Resampling approaches such as
SMOTE~\cite{chawla2002} synthesize minority-class examples to re-balance the
training distribution; modified loss functions such as focal loss~\cite{lin2017}
down-weight easy negatives during training, focusing gradient updates on the
hard examples that most influence the decision boundary. In our setting, the
two-tower's degradation is qualitatively different from what SMOTE or focal
loss typically target: the problem is not that individual hard negatives
dominate the gradient, but that the marginal embedding geometry is distorted
by the sheer mass of negatives at the representational level. Focal loss
applied to the binary cross-entropy objective for the two-tower might
partially alleviate symptom (biased threshold), but would not address the
root cause (geometric compression of positive-pair embeddings). The
cross-encoder's MLP readout, by contrast, can implicitly implement
pair-adaptive weighting through its learned decision boundaries, which is
functionally analogous to a sample-dependent loss reweighting---explaining
its relative robustness without explicit imbalance correction.

\subsection{Ablation: Isolating the Contribution of Joint Encoding}
\label{ssec:ablation}

To determine whether the cross-encoder's AUROC advantage is attributable to
joint encoding per se or to the element-wise product interaction term
($\mathbf{e}_{TF} \odot \mathbf{e}_g$), we trained a \emph{no-product}
cross-encoder variant whose input is
$[\mathbf{e}_{TF} \,|\, \mathbf{e}_g \,|\, \mathbf{x}_{TF} \,|\, \mathbf{x}_g]$
(input dimension $2d + 2m$ instead of $3d + 2m$). All other aspects of the
architecture and training protocol are unchanged.

Table~\ref{tab:ablation} presents the results. The no-product cross-encoder
achieves a mean AUROC of 0.8972 under balanced (1:1) training, compared to
0.9025 for the full cross-encoder and 0.7941 for the two-tower. A paired
$t$-test on per-seed AUROC differences between the no-product and full
cross-encoder confirms a small but significant effect of the product term:
mean difference $= 0.0054$, $t(4) = 4.08$, $p = 0.015$ (two-tailed,
Bonferroni-corrected $\alpha = 0.017$; marginally significant under
correction), Cohen's $d = 1.82$. Note that the no-product cross-encoder has
$\approx$5.32M parameters versus 5.58M for the full cross-encoder and
two-tower; this $\approx$4.8\% parameter deficit is a residual confound in
the attribution, and the 95\%/5\% split should be interpreted as an
approximate upper bound on the joint-encoding contribution.

\textbf{Interpretation.} The element-wise product term contributes
$\approx$0.53~AUROC points of the total 10.84-point gap. Joint
encoding alone (no-product cross-encoder vs.\ two-tower) accounts for the
remaining $\approx$10.31~points (95.1\% of the gap). This result substantially
strengthens C1: the cross-encoder's advantage over the two-tower is
\emph{overwhelmingly} a consequence of joint input processing, not of the
multiplicative inductive bias. The element-wise product provides a small
additional benefit consistent with its role as a feature that amplifies
co-active embedding dimensions~\cite{rendle2010}.

Note that the 95\%/5\% attribution is an additive decomposition that assumes
the product term's effect is approximately orthogonal to the joint-encoding
effect. Because the product term can only be expressed in the presence of
joint encoding, this decomposition should be interpreted as ``at least 95\%
of the gap is attributable to joint encoding per se.''

\subsection{Representational Redundancy via Neuron Pruning (Two-Tower)}
\label{ssec:h3}

Table~\ref{tab:pruning} presents the post-hoc and fine-tuned AUROC retention
across all 13 tested sparsity levels, with mean $\pm$ std across three seeds
(42, 123, 456). Baseline AUROC is $0.8049 \pm 0.0079$ (mean across seeds).

Post-hoc AUROC retention remains at or above 0.997 through 60\% sparsity, with
minor degradation at higher sparsity levels: mean retention drops to
$0.993 \pm 0.001$ at 70\%, $0.984 \pm 0.008$ at 80\%, and
$0.978 \pm 0.015$ at 90\%. The relatively high standard deviation at 90\%
sparsity (0.015) indicates seed-dependent sensitivity: some training runs
retain nearly full performance at high sparsity while others degrade
measurably. Post-hoc retention \emph{increases} marginally above 1.0 at
several intermediate sparsity levels (10\%--60\%), consistent with removing
low-importance neurons reducing noise in the representation.

At 90\% sparsity, 51 neurons per tower are retained and the model size
decreases from 5,581,824 to approximately 4,627K parameters---a reduction of
$\approx$17\%, not 90\%, because the embedding tables ($\approx$4.52M
parameters, 81\% of total) are unchanged. The mean post-hoc AUROC at 90\%
sparsity is 0.787, compared to baseline 0.805---a drop of 1.8 AUROC points.
After 10 epochs of fine-tuning at 90\% sparsity, mean AUROC recovers to
0.810 (retention 1.006), matching and marginally exceeding baseline.

Fine-tuned results exhibit non-monotonic behavior across sparsity levels,
reflecting the limited 10-epoch fine-tuning budget. Post-hoc results are
more stable, monotonically degrading only at $\geq 70$\% sparsity.
Figs.~\ref{fig:pruning} and~\ref{fig:scatter} visualize the
sparsity-retention trade-off and compression-AUROC relationship.

\textbf{Interpretation.} The two-tower model shows substantial representational
redundancy: post-hoc retention remains $\geq 0.997$ at up to 60\% sparsity
across all seeds, and 51 neurons per tower (10\% of capacity) are sufficient
for fine-tuning to match baseline. Three factors plausibly contribute.

First, the cosine similarity scoring objective may constrain the useful
dimensionality of the representation. Since the regulatory score depends only
on the \emph{direction} of the TF and gene encodings, the effective degrees of
freedom available to the objective may be far fewer than 512. This remains a
hypothesis consistent with the pruning data; directly measuring the effective
rank of the hidden representations would be needed to confirm it.

Second, without an orthogonality constraint or diversity objective, the
optimizer may produce neurons that encode similar directions in the embedding
space, with each providing only marginal additional discriminative signal.

Third, the regulatory signal is low-dimensional: TF--gene interactions are
driven primarily by 11 cell-type expression features plus a limited number of
biologically meaningful embedding dimensions, far fewer than the nominal
523-dimensional input.

\subsection{Cross-Encoder Representational Redundancy}
\label{ssec:h3_ce}

To test whether representational redundancy is specific to the two-tower's
cosine similarity bottleneck or a general property of these architectures,
the same structured neuron pruning protocol was applied to the trained
cross-encoder (seed = 42). Baseline AUROC for the cross-encoder pruning
experiment is 0.8924.

Table~\ref{tab:ce_pruning} presents the full results across all 13 sparsity
levels. Several patterns are notable.

\textbf{Post-hoc retention.} The cross-encoder maintains post-hoc AUROC
retention $\geq 0.999$ at all sparsity levels up to 70\%, with retention
actually \emph{increasing} beyond 1.0 at 50\%--70\% sparsity (peak of 1.016
at 70\%). At 80\%, post-hoc retention drops to 0.999 (essentially no
degradation), and at 90\% it falls to 0.987---a meaningful but moderate
drop of 1.3 AUROC points from baseline.

\textbf{Comparison with two-tower.} The cross-encoder is comparable to
or slightly more robust than the two-tower in post-hoc performance at
intermediate sparsity levels, but both architectures degrade at 90\% sparsity.
This finding indicates that \emph{representational redundancy is not exclusive
to cosine similarity scoring}: a cross-encoder with MLP readout is similarly
over-parameterized in its hidden layer. The mechanistic account of the C1
AUROC gap (Section~\ref{ssec:h3}) as arising primarily from the two-tower's
capacity underutilization is therefore not supported by this comparison alone;
both architectures show similar tolerance to neuron pruning, and the AUROC gap
is better attributed to the inductive bias of joint input processing
(Section~\ref{ssec:ablation}).

\textbf{Fine-tuned retention.} Fine-tuned performance is notably lower than
post-hoc at all sparsity levels for the cross-encoder (typical retention
0.96--0.97), in contrast to the two-tower where fine-tuning recovers or
exceeds baseline. Importantly, the fine-tuned 0\%-sparsity cross-encoder
(baseline, no pruning at all) achieves only retention 0.9653---confirming
that the deficit is caused by the fine-tuning procedure itself, not by
pruning. The root cause is the fresh Adam optimizer state used for
fine-tuning: resetting all momentum and variance estimates to zero causes
early Adam steps to behave as approximately sign-gradient steps
(step $\approx \eta \cdot \text{sign}(g)$ before historical statistics
accumulate), which can disrupt a converged model's weight configuration.
The two-tower's cosine similarity mechanism is more robust to such
perturbations than the cross-encoder's learned joint feature interactions.
In the revised implementation, the fine-tuning learning rate for the
cross-encoder has been reduced from 0.001 to 0.0002 and extended to
20 epochs to allow the fresh Adam state to stabilize; results in
Table~\ref{tab:ce_pruning} reflect the original protocol and should be
interpreted accordingly.

\subsection{Classical Baseline Comparison}
\label{ssec:baseline}

To contextualize the neural models' AUROC values, we evaluated three classical
machine learning classifiers on the same test split using \emph{expression-only}
features (no learned embeddings): the 11-dimensional cell-type expression profile
of the TF and the 11-dimensional profile of the gene are concatenated to form a
22-dimensional input.

Table~\ref{tab:baseline} presents the results. A gradient-boosted tree
classifier achieves AUROC~0.587 and accuracy 55.4\%---barely above the
majority-class baseline (AUROC 0.500). These results reveal two important facts.
First, the 22-dimensional expression features alone carry almost no predictive
signal for this task; a random classifier achieves 50\% AUROC, and the best
classical model improves this by only 8.7 AUROC points. Second, this implies
that the neural models' AUROC of 0.7941 (two-tower) and 0.9025 (cross-encoder)
are driven primarily by the 4.52M parameters of the learned entity embeddings
(81\% of total parameters), which encode database membership structure. Note
that expression coverage in this dataset is sparse: only 10.7\% of TFs and
14.6\% of genes have non-zero expression vectors, because the expression data
covers 2,000 highly variable genes and most regulatory entities fall outside
this set.

\textbf{Interpretation.} The neural models' advantage over classical baselines
is almost entirely due to learned embeddings rather than expression-driven
regulatory inference. This confirms the transductive, database-reconstruction
nature of the task (Section~\ref{ssec:data}): the embeddings effectively learn
which entities co-appear in positive training pairs, and generalization to novel
TF--gene pairs is limited by the fixed training vocabulary. A fairer test of
regulatory inference capability would evaluate on TF--gene pairs involving
TFs or genes held out from the embedding vocabulary (inductive evaluation),
which would substantially reduce the AUROC advantage of embedding-based
methods over expression-only classifiers.

\label{sec:discussion}

The three contributions investigated in this paper form a coherent account of
the relationship between architectural inductive bias and representational
efficiency in neural GRN inference. The cross-encoder's advantage over the
two-tower (C1) is robust to moderately imbalanced conditions where the two-tower
fails (C2). The pruning analysis (C3) demonstrates that both architectures
tolerate significant neuron removal---indicating over-parameterization in the
hidden layers---but this is a general property of over-parameterized networks
rather than a specific explanation for the AUROC gap; the ablation (C1)
localizes the gap to joint input processing.

\textbf{Unified interpretation.} The factorized two-tower architecture imposes
a strong inductive bias that is both its strength and its weakness. By
separating TF and gene encoding, the two-tower enables efficient inference at
scale: given pre-computed TF and gene embeddings, all $M \times N$ TF--gene
regulatory scores can be computed as a matrix product in $O(Md + Nd)$ time
rather than the $O(MNd)$ required by per-pair cross-encoder inference. This
scalability property is genuine and relevant for genome-scale GRN inference
over tens of thousands of gene pairs. However, the cosine-similarity scoring
mechanism that enables this efficiency also prevents the model from learning
the joint features that drive regulatory specificity, leading to the AUROC gap
observed in C1 and the sensitivity to class imbalance in C2.

The neuron pruning results (C3) show that both architectures are substantially
over-parameterized in their hidden layers: the two-tower retains $\geq 0.997$
post-hoc AUROC through 60\% sparsity, and the cross-encoder retains $\geq 0.999$
through 70\% sparsity. Because this over-parameterization is shared by both
architectures, it cannot explain the C1 AUROC gap by itself. The ablation
reported in Section~\ref{ssec:ablation} provides the more precise account:
$\approx$95\% of the 10.8-point gap is attributable to joint input processing,
with the remaining $\approx$5\% from the element-wise product. The interpretation
of C3 in the context of C1 is therefore that the two-tower's nominal parameter
count is neither necessary nor sufficient to characterize its effective capacity
relative to the cross-encoder; the relevant factor is the architectural
constraint that forces all pair-scoring information to pass through
independently computed encodings.

\textbf{Temperature sensitivity.} The temperature grid search
(Section~\ref{ssec:tempsearch}) confirms that the two-tower AUROC advantage is
not an artifact of $\tau = 0.05$: the best-performing temperature ($\tau = 1.0$,
AUROC $= 0.8111$) is only 1.1~AUROC points above the default $\tau = 0.05$, and
the gap to the cross-encoder (0.9025) remains large (9.1 points) even at the
optimal temperature. The AUROC gap is robust to temperature tuning.

\textbf{Practical recommendations (qualified to this dataset).}
On the human brain snRNA-seq dataset studied here: if AUROC is the primary
criterion, the cross-encoder (with element-wise product) is the clear choice,
with a 10.8-point AUROC advantage over the parameter-matched two-tower. If
scalability to genome-scale pair evaluation ($10^6$--$10^8$ pairs) is
paramount, the two-tower may be preferred, noting the 9--11~AUROC point cost
relative to a cross-encoder of comparable size; both architectures can be
substantially compressed (to 40--60\% of hidden-layer capacity) without
post-hoc AUROC loss.

\textbf{Limitations.}

\emph{Transductive setting and generalizability.}
Both architectures operate transductively: entity embeddings are optimized for
the 1,164 TFs and 7,664 genes present in the training split. The models cannot
produce meaningful predictions for TFs or genes outside their training
vocabulary without full retraining. The classical baseline comparison
(Section~\ref{ssec:baseline}) shows that expression features alone yield
AUROC~$\approx$0.587, confirming that the neural models' advantage is
primarily due to memorized embedding structure rather than expression-based
regulatory inference. Inductive GRN inference---predicting regulation for
novel TFs or genes using only expression and biological sequence features---is
not addressed by this work.

\emph{Single dataset and scope.}
This study uses a single biological dataset (human brain single-nucleus
RNA-seq); whether the cross-encoder advantage persists across other tissues,
organisms, or expression modalities is not established. Multi-dataset
evaluation on a standardized benchmark such as BEELINE~\cite{pratapa2020}
is a necessary next step to assess generalizability. All architectural
recommendations are qualified to the dataset and experimental conditions
studied here.

\emph{Fairness of the architectural comparison.}
The ablation experiment resolves the element-wise product confound
($\approx$95\% joint encoding, $\approx$5\% product), but the no-product
cross-encoder has $\approx$4.8\% fewer parameters (5.32M vs.\ 5.58M), a
residual confound. The learning rate $\eta = 0.001$ was chosen for
cross-encoder stability rather than from per-architecture grid search; the
remaining gap at optimal temperature ($\tau=1.0$, AUROC $=0.8111$) is still
9.1~points. Independent per-architecture learning-rate tuning remains an
open question.

\emph{Evaluation protocol and class imbalance.}
AUPRC, AUROC, accuracy, and macro F1 are reported; ROC and PR curves for all
four conditions are shown in Figs.~\ref{fig:roc_comparison}
and~\ref{fig:pr_comparison}. The 5:1 negative sampling regime is evaluated
on a balanced (1:1) test set; accuracy and F1 at threshold 0.5 are
therefore affected by miscalibration. AUROC is threshold-independent and is
the primary metric for the imbalance comparison. The 5:1 condition is far
from the true ratio ($\approx$376:1); behavior under severe imbalance
($\geq$50:1) is not established and is a significant limitation for
deployment conclusions.

\emph{Model depth and architecture search.}
Both models use 2-layer MLPs with 512 hidden units. Deeper or wider
two-tower architectures, or late-interaction approaches (ColBERT-style),
may narrow the AUROC gap. A systematic search is beyond the scope of this
study.

\emph{Baseline comparison.}
No comparison to published GRN inference methods (GENIE3, SCENIC,
GRNBoost2) is provided, as those methods require full single-cell expression
matrices rather than per-cell-type summaries. The classical baseline
Table~\ref{tab:baseline} provides expression-only context.
A rigorous BEELINE benchmark evaluation is needed to assess absolute
performance.

\emph{Label quality.}
Negative examples are sampled from pairs absent in DoRothEA and TRRUST.
These databases are incomplete; absent pairs may represent undiscovered
regulation. False-negative contamination is not quantified.

\emph{Pruning experiment.}
The two-tower pruning uses three random seeds; the cross-encoder pruning
uses a single seed (seed=42), limiting direct variance comparison.
The cross-encoder fine-tuning results are affected by a fresh-Adam
disruption artifact (see Section~\ref{ssec:h3_ce}); post-hoc retention
values are the primary comparison metric.

\emph{Implementation.}
All models were implemented in Rust without a reference framework.
Correctness is supported by: (1) training loss decreases monotonically from
$\approx$0.69 to $<$0.40; (2) gradient norms are well-behaved; (3) AUROC
improves monotonically from $\approx$0.5 at initialization.
No independent benchmark replication is provided.

\textbf{Future directions.} Three extensions are directly motivated by these
results. First, an InfoNCE-trained two-tower~\cite{oord2018}, where the
contrastive objective explicitly attracts positive TF--gene pairs and repels
negatives within a batch, may be more robust to the marginal distribution
sensitivity observed in C2 while retaining the two-tower's inference
efficiency. Supervised contrastive learning~\cite{khosla2020} extends this
framework to settings with multiple positives per anchor---potentially
valuable if multiple curated databases (DoRothEA confidence tiers A/B,
TRRUST, ENCODE) are combined as complementary positive-label sources,
providing a more nuanced signal than a single binary label. The InfoNCE
objective may also address the geometric compression problem identified in
C2: by explicitly maximizing the cosine similarity of positive pairs within
each batch, it counteracts the distortion caused by an overabundance of
negatives.
Second, a cross-attention two-tower --- where the gene encoder attends over
the TF encoding before producing a final representation --- would bridge the
two architectures, enabling per-pair interaction features while maintaining
the factorized structure required for efficient inference~\cite{humeau2020}.
Third, evaluation on additional datasets and tissues (e.g., the BEELINE
benchmark~\cite{pratapa2020}) with additional GRN baselines
(GENIE3~\cite{huynh2010}, GRNBoost2~\cite{moerman2019}, PIDC~\cite{chan2017})
would establish whether the architectural advantage observed here generalizes
across tissues, organisms, and expression modalities, and would provide the
absolute performance context needed to assess practical utility.

% ── VI. CONCLUSION ───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

This paper has compared dual-encoder (two-tower) and cross-encoder neural
architectures for TF--gene regulatory edge prediction from single-nucleus
RNA-seq data under parameter-matched, data-matched conditions. The
cross-encoder achieves 10.8~higher AUROC points than the two-tower under
balanced training (0.9025 vs.\ 0.7941, $t(4)=27.08$, $p < 0.0001$,
Bonferroni-corrected $\alpha=0.017$, Cohen's $d=12.1$) and remains stable
under 5:1 negative sampling where the two-tower degrades by 10.1~points
($t(4.25)=4.06$, $p = 0.014$, nominally significant under correction).
A no-product ablation isolates $\approx$95\% of the gap (10.3~AUROC points)
to joint input processing and $\approx$5\% (0.5~points) to the element-wise
interaction term; the no-product model has 4.8\% fewer parameters, a
residual confound. A temperature grid search confirms the gap persists at all
tested temperatures (best two-tower AUROC $= 0.8111$). Structured neuron
pruning across three seeds shows both architectures tolerate hidden-layer
sparsity: the two-tower retains $\geq 0.997$ post-hoc AUROC through 60\%
sparsity; the cross-encoder $\geq 0.999$ through 70\% sparsity. A
classical baseline comparison shows that expression-only features yield
AUROC~$\approx$0.587, confirming that both neural models' advantage is
embedding-driven and that the evaluation measures database reconstruction
in a transductive setting rather than inductive regulatory inference.
On the single human brain dataset studied here, the cross-encoder is the
preferred architecture for discriminative accuracy; both architectures
can be substantially compressed without post-hoc AUROC loss. Multi-dataset
validation, inductive evaluation on held-out entities, and comparison to
published GRN baselines are required before general conclusions can be drawn.
